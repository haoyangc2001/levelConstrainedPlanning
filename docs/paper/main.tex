\begin{abstract}
Keeping a manipulator's end effector level throughout an entire path---the
``carrying a tray of water'' constraint---turns point-to-point planning into
motion on a low-dimensional constraint manifold, where feasible solutions are
scarce and gradient-based optimizers are highly sensitive to their initial
seed. We present a level-constrained trajectory planning system for the SR5
manipulator that (i) constructs a diverse family of rule-based level-preserving
seeds via horizontal-pose interpolation and branch-consistent inverse
kinematics, (ii) repairs and refines every seed with GPU-batched CuRobo
trajectory optimization, and (iii) selects the executable trajectory with a
level-first hard validator. We further wrap this planner in a closed-loop
learning--optimization system---\emph{data generation, model learning,
optimization validation, failure fallback, data update}---in which a diffusion
seed model and a success critic learn the distribution of CuRobo-repairable
seeds, while every learned candidate still returns to CuRobo repair, hard
validation, and rule-based fallback. We report the constrained-planning success
rate under a fixed time budget and analyze the contribution of each component.
\end{abstract}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% Sampling-based constrained planning (TSR/CBiRRT, OMPL constrained, cpRRTC, McVAMP).
% IK-based methods (IKFast, TRAC-IK, RelaxedIK, IKFlow).
% Trajectory optimization (CHOMP, STOMP, TrajOpt, GPMP, CuRobo).
% Learning-based seeding (DiffusionSeeder, Motion Planning Diffusion, PRESTO).
Sampling-based constrained planners represent path constraints explicitly and
often rely on projection to remain on the feasible manifold. Classical
constraint-manifold planners such as CBiRRT/TSR methods
\cite{berenson2009cbirrt} and surveys of constrained sampling
\cite{kingston2018sampling} establish the projection-based view. Recent systems
such as VAMP, pRRTC, cpRRTC, and McVAMP vectorize this family for high-throughput
planning on modern hardware \cite{vamp2024,prrtc2026,cprrtc2025,mcvamp2026}.
These methods are strong references for constraint satisfaction, but their
projection operators are embedded inside search. Our system instead places
manifold awareness before local optimization and uses hard post-verification
afterward, trading projection completeness for a batched optimizer pipeline with
fallback.

Inverse-kinematics methods address a different subproblem: generating joint
solutions for desired tool poses. TRAC-IK and RelaxedIK improve robustness and
real-time feasibility for generic IK \cite{trac-ik,relaxedik}, while IKFlow
models diverse IK solutions and IKLink explicitly reduces configuration
switches along end-effector trajectories \cite{ikflow,iklink2024}. We use IK in
the same spirit, but only as a seed-construction component. The planning problem
still requires full-path continuity, collision checking, motion quality, and
post-optimization validation.

Trajectory-optimization methods such as CHOMP, STOMP, TrajOpt, GPMP2, and
cuRobo optimize smooth collision-aware motion efficiently
\cite{chomp,stomp,trajopt,gpmp2,curobo2023}. They are natural downstream
refiners for dense joint trajectories, but when path constraints are represented
as soft penalties they inherit the degradation and initialization-sensitivity
issues described in Sec.~\ref{sec:problem}. Our contribution is not a new
optimizer. It is a constraint-aware wrapper that constructs manifold-near seeds,
repairs them with a batched optimizer, and separates optimizer convergence from
task success through a hard validator.

Learning-based motion planning and seeding methods use generative models to
accelerate planning. Diffuser and Motion Planning Diffusion model trajectory
distributions for planning with diffusion guidance \cite{diffuser2022,mpd2023};
DiffusionSeeder and PRESTO are closer to our setting because they generate seeds
or key configurations that are then refined by a downstream optimizer
\cite{diffusionseeder2024,presto2024}. Our distinction is the closed-loop
contract: learned samples are candidates only, failures train a separate critic
rather than becoming positive diffusion targets, and a verified rule branch
remains available as fallback. Table~\ref{tab:related} summarizes the resulting
positioning.

\section{Problem Formulation and Structural Limitations}
\label{sec:problem}
% Trajectory as (N+1) x n joint-configuration sequence P = [q_0, ..., q_N].
% Objective: smoothness (velocity/acceleration/jerk) + regularization.
% Constraints: start q_0 = q_start; goal T_ee(q_N) = T_goal;
%   level path constraint z_ee(q_k)^T z_w >= cos(eps) for all k;
%   joint limits; velocity/acceleration; collision d(q_k, O) >= d_safe;
%   joint-space continuity ||q_{k+1} - q_k|| <= dq_max.
We consider a manipulator with $n$ joints and a trajectory
$P=\{\qvec_0,\ldots,\qvec_N\}$, where each waypoint
$\qvec_k\in\mathbb{R}^n$ is a joint configuration. A request specifies the
start configuration $\qvec_s$, a terminal tool pose $\Tgoal$, a workspace
obstacle set $\mathcal{O}$, and an orientation-path constraint that must hold
over the full trajectory, not only at the terminal pose. The optimizer's nominal
objective combines smoothness and regularization terms, while the task-level
constraints require
\begin{align}
  \qvec_0 &= \qvec_s, &
  T_{\mathrm{ee}}(\qvec_N) &\approx \Tgoal, \nonumber\\
  \qvec_k &\in [\qvec_{\min},\qvec_{\max}], &
  d(\qvec_k,\mathcal{O}) &\ge d_{\mathrm{safe}},
  \label{eq:problem-constraints}
\end{align}
along with bounded joint-space continuity and motion-quality proxies such as
velocity, acceleration, and jerk.

The level constraint at each waypoint is expressed as
\begin{equation}
  \zee(\qvec_k)^\top \zw \ge \cos\epsilon, \qquad k = 0,1,\dots,N,
  \label{eq:level}
\end{equation}
where $\zee(\qvec_k)$ is the tool axis and $\zw$ the target world direction.
% Observation 1: soft-penalty constraint degradation.
% Observation 2: non-convex initialization sensitivity.
Eq.~\eqref{eq:level} defines an orientation-path feasible set embedded inside
joint space. When a task constrains one or more tool axes over the entire path,
the feasible set behaves like a thin manifold rather than a full-dimensional
volume. This creates two coupled difficulties. First, random or generic seeds
are unlikely to lie near the manifold. Second, inverse kinematics is
multi-valued: neighboring tool poses can be realized by different joint
branches, so a path that is continuous in task space can still jump in joint
space.

\textbf{Observation 1 (Constraint degradation).} When a path constraint is
encoded only as a weighted penalty in a scalar objective, a local optimum can
reduce total cost while violating the constraint, because constraint
satisfaction is traded against smoothness, goal, collision, and regularization
terms.

\textbf{Observation 2 (Initialization sensitivity).} For a non-convex objective
solved by local descent, the returned trajectory is confined to the basin of its
initial seed. A seed far from the level manifold can converge to a locally
smooth but constraint-violating trajectory.

These observations motivate the system design in Sec.~\ref{sec:method}. Rather
than relying on a single objective weight to enforce the level path constraint,
we construct seeds near the manifold before optimization and apply a hard
post-optimization verifier before any trajectory is counted as successful.

\section{System Design}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Overview: A Verified Self-Improving Loop}
\label{sec:system-overview}
% Pipeline as executor, labeled-data generator, and verifier with fallback.
The system is organized around one invariant: no trajectory is treated as
executable until it has passed the same hard validation gate. The online planner
therefore has three roles. First, it is an executor-facing planner that receives
a start configuration, a goal pose, a world description, and a level constraint.
Second, it is a data generator: every seed, optimizer return code, validation
failure, fallback recovery, and selected trajectory is serialized as a labeled
run artifact. Third, it is a verifier with fallback: learned proposals are never
used directly, but are repaired by the optimizer and then accepted or rejected
by the same hard gate as rule-generated proposals.

Fig.~\ref{fig:system} summarizes this loop. A request first enters the
manifold-aware seed generator, which produces several candidate joint-space
paths near the level manifold. A batch optimizer then refines each seed within
its local basin. The hard validator checks the returned candidates for path
alignment, terminal goal accuracy, continuity, joint limits, dimensioned motion
quality, and world collision. The selector ranks only valid candidates, with
alignment validity as the first key. If the learned branch produces no valid
candidate, the system falls back to the verified rule branch and records the
failure for the next dataset version.

\subsection{Decoupled Constraint Satisfaction}
\label{sec:seeds}
% Cartesian linear interpolation of position; pose restricted to the horizontal
% family via goal-relative twist interpolation Q_i = Q_g * T_y(theta_i);
% branch-consistent IK selection minimizing joint jump + trend + goal-anchor +
% limit-margin costs; smoothed / bridged variants; segmented optimization.
% Batch optimizer repair; level-first hard gate and selection.
The decoupling step moves level satisfaction out of a single soft penalty and
into two explicit stages: pre-optimization seed construction and post-
optimization verification. This targets the two structural limitations from
Sec.~\ref{sec:problem}. The pre-seeding stage addresses initialization
sensitivity by placing the optimizer near the level manifold before local
descent begins. The hard gate addresses constraint degradation by refusing to
equate optimizer convergence with task success.

For each request, the rule seed constructor interpolates the Cartesian position
from the start tool pose to the goal while restricting the orientation path to a
level-preserving family. Let $Q_g$ be the target orientation and let $T_y(\theta)$
denote a twist about the tool axis that preserves the level direction. The
candidate orientation sequence is parameterized as
$Q_i = Q_g T_y(\theta_i)$, where different schedules for $\theta_i$ form a
small family of goal-relative level paths. Each waypoint is then mapped to joint
space by inverse kinematics. Because the same end-effector pose can have
multiple IK branches, the constructor chooses a branch sequence by minimizing a
joint-space cost that penalizes jumps from the previous waypoint, trend
reversals, distance from a goal-anchor solution, and proximity to joint limits.
The raw path is supplemented with smoothed and bridge-repaired variants so the
optimizer receives multiple nearby basins rather than a single brittle seed.

The batch optimizer is used only as a repair and refinement operator. It
receives seed trajectories and returns candidate paths, but the downstream gate
defines success. The selector first discards candidates whose hard validator
fails. Among valid candidates it uses a level-first ordering: smaller maximum
alignment deviation, smaller start-joint gap, smaller joint-step jump, smoother
twist evolution, and finally lower path cost. This ordering keeps the task
constraint ahead of secondary smoothness or objective values.

\subsection{From a Reliable Planner to a Data Engine}
\label{sec:data-engine}
% Planner limitations become data-engine motivation: serial IK cost and manually
% bounded rule coverage; every run emits labeled success/failure artifacts.
The rule branch is reliable but not free. Its IK search is serial along the
path, and its coverage is bounded by manually designed orientation schedules and
branch-selection heuristics. These limitations are exactly what make the planner
useful as a data engine. Every successful run provides a repairable seed and a
validated trajectory. Every failed run provides a structured counterexample:
alignment failure, joint discontinuity, collision, motion-quality violation,
optimizer failure, or fallback recovery. The learning problem is therefore not
to imitate an optimal trajectory in isolation, but to learn which seed
distributions are repairable by the verified downstream pipeline.

\subsection{Self-Improving Closed Loop}
\label{sec:closedloop}
% Diffusion seed model learns p(tau_seed | q_s, T_g, O, C); success critic
% predicts (P_success, alignment risk, collision risk, opt cost); every learned
% candidate returns to optimizer repair + hard validation; failures fall back to
% rule seeds; all outcomes feed the next dataset version.
The learned branch models a conditional distribution over seed trajectories,
$p(\tau_{\mathrm{seed}}\mid \qvec_s,\Tgoal,\mathcal{O},\mathcal{C})$, where
$\mathcal{O}$ is the obstacle description and $\mathcal{C}$ is the active
constraint class. The diffusion model operates in normalized joint-trajectory
space and is trained on repairable examples rather than on all optimizer
outputs. Its loss combines trajectory reconstruction with level-aware terms, so
generated seeds are encouraged to stay near the same orientation-path manifold
used by the rule branch. At inference time, multiple independent denoising
samples provide a candidate set that can cover several local basins.

Failures are not mixed into the diffusion target. A failed trajectory can be
informative, but treating it as a positive demonstration would increase the
probability of sampling invalid seeds. Instead, failures train a success critic
that predicts whether a generated seed is likely to repair successfully and
estimates risks such as alignment error, collision exposure, and optimizer
effort. The critic is a filter and ordering module: it selects which learned
candidates spend repair budget first, while the hard gate remains the final
authority.

The fallback contract separates safety from efficiency. Under an additive
budget, the system can try learned candidates first and then invoke the verified
rule branch if none pass; in that budget semantics, learning provides an
opportunity for improvement without removing the rule baseline floor. Under a
fixed wall-clock or fixed solve-call budget, the learned attempt consumes budget
and can trade off against the rule branch. We therefore report fixed-budget
success as an empirical efficiency question rather than as a guaranteed lower
bound. This distinction is central to the paper narrative: the architecture is
verified and fallback-safe, while the hypothesis that learned seeds cover the
optimizer's convergence basins more efficiently must be tested.
